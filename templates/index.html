Richard Phillips Feynman

THE GREAT EXPLAINER

1918

Richard Phillips Feynman burst into the world in Far Rockaway, Queens, like a proton escaping a cyclotron. His father, a uniform salesman with a penchant for teaching his son to “question the ritual,” filled young Dick’s head with patterns: “See that bird? It’s a Spinkius spinkius in every language, but the names don’t matter—what’s it doing?” By 10, Feynman had a “lab” in his closet, rewiring busted radios for neighbors. When one repair ended with the house nearly burning down, he shrugged: “I guessed the wiring.” A harbinger of things to come.

1943

So there I was at Los Alamos, babysitting bomb math and cracking safes for kicks. Security? Pfft. I figured out guys used their wife’s birthdays as combos. One colonel’s safe had “25-25-25”—three times his anniversary! I left a note: “GUESS WHO.” Bet they blamed the commies. Oh, and I taught myself to pick locks and mimic a Los Alamos accent. Once convinced a guard I was a “foreigner” from New Mexico. “¡Vámonos, amigo!” Physics? Easy. People? Hilarious

1965

The Nobel Committee called. “You’ve won for QED!” I groaned. “Do I have to accept?” They insisted. So I showed up in a borrowed tux, gave a speech about how awards are “a pain in the neck,” then partied with Swedish students who taught me a chant: “Yum-yum-yum, Feynman’s a bum!” Later, a reporter asked, “What’s your secret?” I said, “I ignore experts. And carry a notebook to doodle anything—even failed ideas.” Then I flew to Rio to teach physics in Portuguese. Why? Porque não?

1986

The Challenger exploded. NASA swore it wasn’t the O-rings. Feynman, sipping ice water in a Senate hearing, raised an eyebrow. “Let’s do an experiment.” He scribbled in his report: “For a successful technology, reality must take precedence over public relations.” NASA squirmed. Feynman grinned. The press dubbed him “The Great Explainer.” He preferred “That guy who out-stubborned a bureaucracy

1988

So the cancer got me. Big deal. I told the docs, “Dying’s boring. Let’s talk quantum spin!” My last words? “I’d hate to die twice. It’s so boring.” They put “He loved science” on my tombstone. Meh. Should’ve been: “Here lies Feynman. Finally stopped asking questions.” (Spoiler: He didn’t.)
